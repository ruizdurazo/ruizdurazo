> Note: This is mostly focused on “products” in the tech sector (i.e. software development where design, engineering, and management will be involved). They may apply elsewhere too.

## Intro

These are the most important things I know about teamwork and building products. Teams that follow these lessons work smoothly and actually get ~~shit~~ *ship* done. 🚢

## Key Lessons (Very Summarized)

Part 1: Planning, deciding, estimating.

1. **Planning matters. The plan doesn’t.**
2. **If you build it, you’ll have 2 choices: Kill it or maintain it.**
3. **Time and resources are scarce. They are not neutral.**

Part 2: Grinding, building, shipping.

4. **You are dumb. Try to be less dumb**.
5. **Show, don’t tell. Build it. Fix it. Make it real. Make it demo-able.**
6. **Keep it simple and clean up**.

Part 3: Meetings, communication, coordination.

7. **Communicate effectively. Meetings & Writing. Commit.**
8. **Talk to users. But don’t listen to them**.
9. **Measure things. But don’t chase metrics**.

Part 4: Winning.

10. **Create a launch cycle**.
11. **Do everything and you’ll win**.

## Key Lessons (Less Summarized)

Part 1: Planning, deciding, estimating.

1. **Planning matters. The plan doesn’t.** You have to have a battle plan. You’re the coach and you have to call a play on the field. So plan ahead. The act of planning forces you (and your team) to **prioritize**, **decide**, and find what’s most **urgent** and **valuable**. Have a concrete positive vision for the future. Have an opinion. If your team is big enough, have someone (CEO or similar) look into the future of the product and the future needs of customers, and have someone else (manager) look into the day-to-day running of the product (maintenance, support). Otherwise, you’ll force builders into only doing banal day-to-day tasks or maintenance, and they will lose interest, burn out, or simply quit. 
2. **If you build it, you’ll have 2 choices: Kill it or maintain it**. Anyone can build. That’s not that special anymore. Everybody wants to build something new, launch the new thing, cut the ribbon. But almost no one cares about maintaining things. Maintenance is incredibly important. If you build something, you *WILL* have to decide if you’ll maintain it, kill it, or just let it decay and rot. Therefore, you’re only practical choices are: kill it or maintain it. Make sure what you build is worth your time now and in the future. Make sure it’s valuable. Make sure it’s worth it. Build things you’re willing to maintain. And if you’re not willing to maintain it, kill it ASAP. Otherwise you’re available development resources will simply decay into maintaining a bunch of random, useless things, and your product will ossify. Builders will hate it, and quit. Customers will notice, and jump ship. **“Deciding what not to do is as important as deciding what to do. That’s true for companies, and it’s true for products”** — Steve Jobs
3. **Time and resources are scarce. They are not neutral**. Understand time, planning, and complexity. Time is valuable. Think about opportunity cost. Think about Andy Grove. Sure, you can do many things. But what is the absolute best and highest leverage things you could be doing with your time? “What are the most important problems in your field and why aren’t you working on them?” — Richard Hamming. Understand that there are no refunds for time spent. Don’t waste time and energy on pointless meetings, doing pointless tasks, maintaining useless things, or LARPing. Estimate time accurately. This is crucial for planning too. Respect the base case and consider the worst case, and don’t solely rely on the best case. Time is not neutral. It is either your enemy or your ally. Make sure that time is on your side. 

Part 2: Grinding, building, shipping.

4. **You are dumb. Try to be less dumb**. Your starting point is dumb. Your specs are dumb. Your solution is dumb. Everyone is dumb. No one is an expert. Question everything. Question and understand where the status quo came from. Everything is wrong. Just try to be less wrong. Be open and learn. Try to improve step by step. Try different things. Delete things. Start from scratch. Consider the ambitious. Consider the absurd. 
5. **Show, don’t tell. Build it. Fix it. Make it real. Make it demo-able**. Default to action. If you want to bring up a problem or idea, prove you already thought about it or tried to solve it, and if you didn’t, why the f*ck not. It’s probably just a couple prompts away. If you want to discuss something, bring the draft, sketches, mockups, prototypes, not just words. Discuss early, discuss problems, discuss solutions, design thoroughly, and have detailed specs. Discuss if the problem is correct, discuss if the solution is correct. Align the team, and go from there. Hand-wavy specs and rough ideas are a red flag. Charlatans and bullshitters should be punished or dismissed.
6. **Keep it simple and clean up**. Simple = good. Complicated = bad. Friction is bad. For consumers, if it’s not simple, it’s probably a shit solution anyway. Go back to the drawing board. Say no to feature creep. Wiggins’ Law: If it’s hard (daunting), cut scope; else, execute. Hard things are hard to build and even harder to maintain. Once it works, it’s usually better to not add anything than to add anything at all. It’s even better to delete things than to add things. Deleting doesn’t make things worse. Often, it makes things better. “Entropy erodes everything including software.” — Ivan Zhao, Founder & CEO Notion

Part 3: Meetings, communication, coordination.

7. **Communicate effectively. Meetings & Writing. Commit.** Write specs. Write updates. Write notes. Establish what effective communication will mean for the team. Set expecations. Make people sign up and fully commit. Don’t force anyone to do things they don’t want to (you’ll drive away talent, or they’ll turn into passive *agents*). Communicate the why. Communicate proactively, and **write things down**. Do kick-offs and 1-on-1’s. Daily standups and large group meetings are a pointless waste of time. If there’s nothing to talk about, cancel the meeting. Lastly, own it. Humility and ownership matters. If there are issues, leaders and team members must admit stupidity and failure, and fix it fast.
8. **Talk to customers. But don’t listen to them**. Customers sort of know their problems, frustrations, and desires. But their ideas and solutions are usually terrible. Their taste is even worse. They don’t know what they want. They’re coming to you for the solution, aren’t they? Your judgement and taste matters. Be dramatically better than the status quo and users’ expectations. Delight users. Be magical. “**People don’t know what they want until you show it to them. That’s why I never rely on market research. Our task is to read things that are not yet on the page.**” — Steve Jobs
9. **Measure things. But don’t chase metrics**. Metrics are not goals. Leaders with zero or poor taste will gravitate towards single metrics and defend themselves by saying they’re “data-driven”. But in reality what they’ll end up doing is hacking the metrics to make themselves look good or smart, and if that fails, they’ll abdicate responsibility using numbers to lie or hide their mistakes and incompetence behind more complicated metrics. Responsibility and taste matter more. Focus on process, direction, momentum, and cutting out distractions. The score takes care of itself. Metrics are not goals. **Metrics can be tracked, but they should never be *desired**.*

Part 4: Winning.

10. **Create a launch cycle**. Launch as many times and as often as possible. Either constant micro launches or changelogs (monthly or quarterly or whatever). Or have a yearly product/content cycle. (David Sacks’ “The Cadence”) launch often, then launch big once, twice, or thrice a year (à la Apple). Anything to give you momentum. Anything to let users know you’re alive and making things better. Anything to keep you in the news feed and pumping out content. Launch, launch, and launch again. And even if a launch fails to gain traction, launch again and again and again. 🚀 (Pro tip: don’t forget lessons 2 and 3)
11. **Do everything and you’ll win**. Push the pareto front. Trade-offs are everywhere. Cost, time, performance, feature-completeness, complexity, margins, quality, style. Most people struggle to work with multi-variable optimization problems, but most product development is exactly that! Most people might make the wrong tradeoffs, or optimize the wrong thing. But if you’re ruthless and determined, you do everything. If you know what users want, if you keep it simple, and if you’re not dumb, then you can probably afford to do everything within your control at a world-class level, and success will likely be inevitable. While most will just optimize their product for one or two variables (at most), if you do three or more well, you’ll be unstoppable. If you do everything, you’ll win.
